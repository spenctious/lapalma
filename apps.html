Routing apps

The route downloads on this site are designed to be used with dedicated outdoor routing apps that use offline maps, they won't work with more common mapping apps like Google Maps as these need a phone signal to work. There are many apps available and many of them are free (at least in the basic version).

Most apps will try to push you to buy their maps but Open StreetMap maps are free and work perfectly well with these routes. Some apps will give you a choice of free maps based on Open StreetMap data.

Getting started

App popularity changes all the time so start by checking out recent reviews and perhaps download a few apps to see which ones you get on with. Most apps make money from map sales or premium subscriptions so check there are decent free maps available. Download one of the routes from this site and check you can get the app to see it and you can get the maps needed for the walk without too much difficulty.

The author's favorite app is OsmAnd which started as an Android app but is available for iOS too now. Its maps are very compact so you can download the whole of the Canary Islands in one go. You can also change the map appearance to your liking and get it to highlight all the waymarked trails.

Whichever app you go for you are likely to have to spend some time working out how to get the maps and get a downloaded route into the app. It's an initial barrier to get over so try to figure it out before you leave home.

Download formats

Nearly all apps use the standard GPX format but there are exceptions. For viewing a route on Google Earth (useful for satellite views of climbs) the KLM format is used. A few other apps such as Maps.me also support this format.

To view routes in Google Earth just open the site (desktop version) and drag a downloaded route onto the map.

Limitations and cautions

GPS apps make trail navigation easy but its not foolproof. To be safe take note of the following points:

  • Always carry a paper map and compass with you as backup and be sufficiently aware of where you are that you could navigate with them if you had to.
  • Fully charge your phone before you leave for the trail and if you're not sure the battery will last the day take a portable charger with you. If you're using your phone as your camera then you should certainly take one on longer walks.
  • Don't assume GPS is pinpoint accurate. If you're not sure about which path to take, choose the most likely and head a short way down it before checking again. It should then be clear if you've chosen the right way or not.
  • Expect to loose the signal sometimes. A GPS device will loose the signal when it doesn't have a clear view of the sky such as when you are in a deep barranco. If your phone looses the signal when it shouldn't, try turning location finding off and on again (in your phone's settings).
  • Don't follow GPS blindly If the path is clearly becoming dangerous, reconsider your route. Don't forget that paths are sometimes rerouted for safety and the GPS route might not be completely upto- date.
