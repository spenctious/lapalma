The official trail network

The network of waymarked trails is organized into three main categories designated GR, PR or SL according to length and are numbered in a roughly anticlockwise order starting from Santa Cruz. The signing and route marking conforms to the European Ramblers' Association (ERA) guidelines so should be readily familiar to anyone who has walked such trails elsewhere in Europe.

Trails are marked with two-colour blazes sited on walls, rocks, and sometimes the ground (but not on trees in areas prone to fire) according to their designation:

GR (Gran Recorrido - Grand Tour)
These are trails over 50 km intended as multi- day hikes. There are two of these on the island, the GR 130 coastal route and the GR 131 which takes a high line along the crater rim and down the island's spine. The two meet at Mirador el Time near Puerto de Tazacorte and Fuencaliente in the south.

These are trails that can supposedly all be completed within a day, though you'd have to be pretty fit to complete some of them in that time. The majority of the island's trails have a PR designation.
SL (Senderos Locales - Local Trails)
These are shorter linking paths that can be used as shortcuts, alternative routes and minor excursions. SL paths have recently been renamed with the middle letters changed to reflect the locality of the walk. There may be old signs or other references that have not been updated so ignore the middle letters and just go by the numbers which have not changed.

Trail closures

If a trail is closed for any reason a sign will be put in place at the trail end points. This is a dispiriting thing to come across when you turn up for a walk so to avoid this situation check the trail status before you set out. Try to find out the cause and extend of a trail closure before abandoning a walk as sometimes only part of a path is closed for maintenance and your walk may not be affected. The closure signs on the trail never give any information but the website usually has some indication why the route is closed (though never really enough).

Other waymarked trails

In addition to the formal network there are a few other waymarked trails on the island which use a simpler, older style of signage but stick to the two-colour style of blazes along the trail.

There is nearly always a marker somewhere near a junction so if you head off and don't see one fairly soon you may have gone the wrong way. If in doubt you can retrace your steps as it is sometimes easier to spot the marker when coming from the reverse direction.

Unmarked trails

There are many other unsigned paths on the island many of which are worth exploring. These paths can be usefully linked in with the trail network to form convenient circuits, connect featured walks and make shortcuts. Forestry paths are broad, easy to follow, and maintained by the forestry department, other paths will vary enormously and may be hard to follow and rather overgrown if not frequented by locals. GPS will help a lot in navigating these paths.
